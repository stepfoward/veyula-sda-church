Kanisa la Waadventista wa Sabato (SDA) lilianzishwa mwishoni mwa karne ya 19 nchini Marekani. Historia yake inahusisha harakati za kidini zilizojitokeza kati ya miaka ya 1820 na 1840, zikiongozwa na watu kama William Miller, ambaye alihubiri kwamba kurudi kwa Yesu Kristo kungetokea karibu mwaka 1844. Mwaka huo, ambao ulikuwa unajulikana kama "Mwaka wa Utabiri," ulipita bila kurudi kwa Kristo, tukio ambalo lilijulikana kama "kutokukamilika kwa unabii." Baada ya hayo, kikundi cha waumini walioitwa "Waongofu wa Pili" waliamini kwamba tarehe iliyokuwa imetabiriwa ilikuwa sahihi lakini tukio lililotokea mbinguni ilikuwa tofauti na waliyotarajia. Kati yao alikuwa Ellen G. White, ambaye alikuwa na maono na mafunuo ambayo yalisaidia kujenga msingi wa imani ya Waadventista wa Sabato. Mafundisho makuu ya Kanisa la Waadventista wa Sabato yanaangazia sabato kama siku ya pili ya Sabato, kufuata afya njema, na imani katika kurudi kwa Kristo. Kanisa hilo lilikua haraka, likianzisha shule, vituo vya afya, na huduma za kijamii. Leo, Kanisa la Waadventista wa Sabato ni moja ya madhehebu makubwa duniani, likiwa na waumini wengi katika nchi nyingi. The Seventh-day Adventist Church

The Seventh-day Adventist Church, often referred to as the "world church," is a global Christian denomination with millions of members in over 200 countries and territories around the world. It is known for its emphasis on healthful living, mission outreach, and the belief in the imminent second coming of Jesus Christ. The world church is organized into local churches, conferences, unions, and divisions, with the General Conference serving as the highest governing body. The church's headquarters is located in Silver Spring, Maryland, USA.
One of the distinctive features of the Seventh-day Adventist Church is its belief in the importance of keeping the seventh-day Sabbath (Saturday) holy, in accordance with the fourth commandment of the Bible. This belief sets it apart from many other Christian denominations that observe Sunday as the Sabbath.
The church operates a large network of schools, hospitals, and humanitarian agencies around the world. It is also known for its emphasis on education, with many Adventist schools and universities offering a holistic approach to learning that includes spiritual, mental, and physical development.
In addition to its focus on health and education, the Seventh-day Adventist Church is also involved in various community service initiatives, disaster relief efforts, and evangelistic outreach programs. Through these efforts, the church seeks to fulfill its mission of sharing the love of Jesus and preparing people for His soon return.
Seventh-day Adventists believe in the importance of living healthy, balanced lives. They promote a vegetarian diet, abstaining from alcohol, tobacco, and other harmful substances. Many Adventists also emphasize the importance of regular exercise and rest. The church places a strong emphasis on mission and evangelism, believing that it has a responsibility to share its message with the world. This is done through various means, including literature distribution, media ministry, and personal evangelism efforts. Seventh-day Adventists also have a strong focus on community service and humanitarian aid. They believe in helping those in need and often engage in activities such as feeding the hungry, providing shelter to the homeless, and offering assistance to disaster victims. Overall, the Seventh-day Adventist Church is a global community of believers who are committed to living out their faith in practical ways. Through their emphasis on health, education, mission, and service, Adventists seek to make a positive impact on the world around them while preparing for the soon return of Jesus Christ.

Ellen G. White

Ellen G. White was a co-founder of the Seventh-day Adventist Church and is considered one of the church's most influential leaders. She was born in 1827 in Gorham, Maine, USA, and from a young age, she was known for her deep faith and strong convictions. White experienced a series of visions and dreams throughout her life, which she believed were messages from God. These visions guided and shaped the beliefs and practices of the early Adventist movement. She wrote extensively about her visions and the insights she gained from them, producing more than 5,000 articles and 40 books. White's writings cover a wide range of topics, including health, education, prophecy, and Christian living. Her most famous books include "Steps to Christ," "The Great Controversy," and "The Desire of Ages," which are still widely read by Seventh-day Adventists today. White's influence extended beyond the Seventh-day Adventist Church. Her teachings on health and lifestyle have had a lasting impact on the broader health reform movement, and her emphasis on the importance of education led to the establishment of a global network of Adventist schools and universities. Ellen G. White passed away in 1915, but her legacy continues to shape the beliefs and practices of the Seventh-day Adventist Church. She is regarded by Adventists as a prophetess, and her writings are considered inspired and authoritative by many members of the church.

William Miller

William Miller was a prominent figure in the early 19th-century American religious scene, known for his involvement in the Millerite movement. Born in 1782 in Massachusetts, Miller was a farmer and self-taught preacher. Miller became convinced through his study of the Bible that the second coming of Jesus Christ was imminent and would occur around the year 1843 or 1844. He based this belief on his interpretation of prophecies in the books of Daniel and Revelation, particularly the prophecy of the "2300 days" in Daniel 8:14. Miller began to publicly preach his beliefs in the 1830s, gaining a following of thousands of people who became known as Millerites. His message attracted widespread attention, and as the predicted date of Christ's return approached, anticipation and excitement grew among his followers. However, the anticipated return of Christ did not occur as expected on the specified dates. This event, known as the "Great Disappointment," occurred in 1844 when October 22, the last date set by Miller, passed without incident. Despite the disappointment, some of Miller's followers continued to believe in his teachings, leading to the formation of various groups, including the Seventh-day Adventist Church, which emerged from the Millerite movement. Miller himself withdrew from public life after the Great Disappointment and died in 1849. Miller is remembered as a significant figure in American religious history, known for his fervent preaching and his role in sparking a movement that would later give rise to several religious denominations. His legacy also includes the lessons learned from the Great Disappointment, which led to a reevaluation of prophetic interpretation and a greater emphasis on spiritual readiness among believers.
